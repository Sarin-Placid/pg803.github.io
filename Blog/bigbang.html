With that Introduction out of the way, let's dive in to it. First off, lets make it clear what exactly the Big Bang is. The Big Bang Theory is a theory that describes how the universe expanded from an initial state of high density and temperature. It is the most widely accepted cosmological model and it explains the evolution of the observable universe from very early on from its creation.

It is popular as it explains a lot of observed phenomena: The abundance of light elements, the Cosmic microwave background radiation and large scale structure are some examples. critically, it agrees with the Hubble-Lemaître law, which is the observation that galaxies are moving away from Earth, and the further away they are, the faster they are receding.

The Big Bang theory depends on two assumptions to work: The universality of physical laws and the cosmological principle. The universality of physical laws is one of the crucial principles of the theory of relativity. The cosmological principle states that the universe, on large scales, is homogeneous and isotropic, or in other words, it appears the same in all directions no matter what the location is. And we know these assumptions are most likely true: The first assumption has been tested by observations showing that the largest possible change in the fine-structure constant over the age of the universe is of order 10^-5. Also, General Relativity has passed many difficult tests over the years. And since the universe appears isotropic from Earth, if the Copernician principle, which states that there is no special observer or vantage point, the universe may indeed be isotropic, which confirms the cosmological principle.

Fine-structure constant: it shows the strength of the electromagnetic interaction between elementary charged particles

When it comes to the expansion of space, general relativity describes spacetime by a metric, which determines the distances that separate nearby "points", which are specified using a coordinate chart. The cosmological principle singles out the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker metric, which describes a homogeneous, isotropic, expanding (or otherwise, contracting) universe. The metric contains a "scale factor" which describes how the universe's size changes with time. it allows the creation of a coordinate system called comoving coordinates, where the grid expands with the universe. Also, a common misunderstanding, the Big Bang is not an explosion in space, but rather an expansion of space. matter does not move outward to fill an empty universe, space itself expands and increases the physical distances between comoving points.

Another important feature is Horizons. Since the universe has a finite age, and light travels at a finite speed, there may be events in the past whose light has not yet had time to reach us. This places a limit on the most distant objects that can be observed. Also, because space is expanding, and more distant objects are receding ever more quickly, light emitted by these objects will never reach us.

Timeline Of The Early Universe

Singularity - Where relativity breaks down

Seeing the expansion of the universe backwards in time using general relativity, we end up with infinite density and temperature at a point in the past. This gravitational singularity shows that general relativity is not an adequate description of the laws of physics in these situations. A unified field theory unifying gravity with the other three interactions would be needed to describe these situations. The age of the universe has been found as 13.8 billion years based on measurements of the universe's expansion using Type Ia supernovae and measurements of temperature fluctuations in the cosmic microwave background.

Despite being extremely dense at this time, the universe did not re-collapse into a singularity. Since the early universe did not immediately collapse into many black holes, matter at that time must have been very evenly distributed with a very small density gradient.

Cosmic Inflation and baryogenesis - The universe grows

The early phases of the universe are subject to much speculation, as we have no observational data on it. Most cosmological models say that the universe had a very high energy density, temperature and pressure, and was rapidly expanding and cooling. The planck epoch (0 to 10^-43 seconds into the expansion of the universe) was a time when the four fundamental forces of the universe - Electromagnetism, The strong and weak nuclear forces, and gravity - were unified as one. The size of the universe at this time was roughly 10^-35 metres, a planck length, and had a temperature of 10^32 degrees Celsius. The conditions were so extreme at the time that even the concept of a particle breaks down. A theory of quantum gravity will be needed to explain this period of time.

at 10^-43 seconds, the grand unification epoch began, with the force of gravity separating from the other forces as the universe's temperature fell. at around 10^-37 seconds, a phase transition (A transition between the states of matter) caused cosmic inflation , where the size of the universe grew exponentially, not stopped by the speed of light, and temperatures dropped by a factor or 100,000. Tiny quantum fluctuations caused by Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, which says that the more accurately you measure the position of a particle, the less accurate its momentum will be, were amplified and would eventually form the large scale structures of the universe.

at around 10^-36 seconds, the strong nuclear force separated from the other forces, and inflation stopped at around 10^-32 seconds, with the universe having increased in size by a factor of at least 10^78. The universe reheated till elementary particles like quark-gluon plasma could be produced, and particle-antiparticle pairs were constantly being produced and destroyed. many exotic particles like W± and Z bosons were formed at this time. At some point, an unknown reaction called baryogenesis caused all antimatter to be annihilated and left a very small excess of quarks and leptons - one in 30 million. This is why we find no antimatter in the present day universe.

Cooling - The universe starts to take its present shape

This is a period of the universe that is much less speculative, as particle energies drop to values that can be achieved in particle accelerators. Phase transitions put the fundamental forces into their present forms, with the electromagnetic force and the weak nuclear force separating at 10^-12 seconds. at 10^-6 seconds, quarks and gluons combined to form baryons like protons and neutrons. The temeperature was no longer high enough to create particle-antiparticle pairs. immediately after this, as mass annihilation occured, leaving just one in 10^8 particles and no antiparticles, with a similar process occuring at 1 second for electrons and positrons. after this, the energy density of the universe was dominated by photons, with few neutrinos.
